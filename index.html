§ 01

The Attack at the Potato Patch

On the morning of August second, Spencer Horner walked out to meet his neighbors. He was fifty-six, a man of Irish and Cherokee blood who stood for the Union in a county that had largely turned its back on it. He raised his hands in surrender. They shot him where he stood.

His son, William Riley, attempted to flee. He was caught in the lot, beaten with pistols, and left for dead. He survived eight days in a hidden cave, fed by women who disguised their supplies as bundles for an infant, before succumbing to his wounds on August 10.

"Wm. R. Homer Born Aug. 1, 1834 Killed Aug. 10, 1864 by parties known to his family."

— Tombstone Inscription, Moore Cemetery

§ 02

The Six Deaths of 1864

In the span of 163 days, the Horner family was decimated by war, disease, and murder. None fell in active combat; all fell to the conditions of the conflict.

§ 03

The Reckoning

When the law refused to act—led by a Sheriff who simultaneously served as a Confederate Colonel—Dr. John Turner Horner took the justice of the hills into his own hands. Leading a squad of men back into the timber, they sought those "known to the family."
